A simple derivation of the Woodbury matrix identity

1 February 2026math
\gdef\M{\v{M}} \gdef\A{\v{A}} \gdef\U{\v{U}} \gdef\C{\v{C}} \gdef\V{\v{V}} \gdef\x{\v{x}} \gdef\y{\v{y}} \gdef\u{\v{u}} \gdef\a{\v{v}}

Problem formulation

Let M=A+UCV\M = \A + \U\C\VM=A+UCV with

  • A∈Mn×n(R)\A \in \mathcal{M}_{n \times n}(\mathbb{R})A∈Mn×n​(R)
  • U∈Mn×m(R)\U \in \mathcal{M}_{n \times m}(\mathbb{R})U∈Mn×m​(R) m<nm<nm<n
  • C∈Mm×m(R)\C \in \mathcal{M}_{m\times m}(\mathbb{R})C∈Mm×m​(R)
  • V∈Mm×n(R)\V \in \mathcal{M}_{m\times n}(\mathbb{R})V∈Mm×n​(R)

We are interested in finding M−1\M^{-1}M−1, but supposing we already know A−1\A^{-1}A−1, we do not want to compute a full n×nn\times nn×n matrix inverse. The Woodbury matrix identity is useful in this case:

(A+UCV)−1=A−1−A−1U(C−1+VA−1U)−1VA−1(\A + \U\C\V)^{-1} = \A^{-1} - \A^{-1}\U(\C^{-1} + \V\A^{-1}\U)^{-1}\V\A^{-1}(A+UCV)−1=A−1−A−1U(C−1+VA−1U)−1VA−1

The only inverse we have to compute are C−1\C^{-1}C−1 and (C−1+VA−1U)−1(\C^{-1} + \V\A^{-1}\U)^{-1}(C−1+VA−1U)−1 which is a m×mm \times mm×m matrix.

Derivation

For example let say we want to invert (I+uv⊤)(\v{I} + uv^\top)(I+uv⊤) it's a n×nn \times nn×n matrix but invertig it is very simple:

(I+uv⊤)x=yx+uv⊤x=yx=y−uv⊤x\begin{align*} (\v{I} + \u\a^\top)\x &= \y \\ \x + \u\a^\top \x &= \y \\ \x &= \y - \u\a^\top \x \tag{1}\\ \end{align*}(I+uv⊤)xx+uv⊤xx​=y=y=y−uv⊤x​(1)​

v⊤xv^\top \xv⊤x is a scalar; it should be easy to calculate. Indeed

v⊤x=v⊤y−v⊤uv⊤x(1+v⊤u)v⊤x=v⊤yv⊤x=v⊤y1+v⊤u\begin{align*} \a^\top \x &= \a^\top \y - \a^\top \u\a^\top \x \\ (1 + \a^\top \u) \a^\top \x &= \a^\top \y \\ \v{v}^\top \x &= \frac{\a^\top \y}{1 + \a^\top \u} \end{align*}v⊤x(1+v⊤u)v⊤xv⊤x​=v⊤y−v⊤uv⊤x=v⊤y=1+v⊤uv⊤y​​

And substituding v⊤x\a^\top \xv⊤x in (1)(1)(1) we get:

x=y−uv⊤1+v⊤uy=(I−uv⊤1+v⊤u)y\x = \y - \frac{\u\a^\top}{1 + \a^\top \u} \y = \left(\v{I} - \frac{\u\a^\top}{1 + \a^\top \u}\right) \yx=y−1+v⊤uuv⊤​y=(I−1+v⊤uuv⊤​)y

Thus (I+u⊤v)−1=I−uv⊤1+v⊤u(\v{I} + \u^\top \a)^{-1} = \v{I} - \frac{\u\a^\top}{1 + \a^\top \u}(I+u⊤v)−1=I−1+v⊤uuv⊤​. We did not have to compute any n×nn \times nn×n matrix inverse (just a 1×11 \times 11×1 one 😉). We will now generalize this result by deriving the Woodbury matrix identity in its full glory. As you will see, the derivation is rather similar to the demonstration of the simple case. We will follow essentially the same steps.

(A+UCV)x=yAx+UCVx=yx+A−1UCVx=A−1yx=A−1y−A−1UCVx\begin{align} (\A + \U\C\V) \x &= \y \notag \\ \A\x + \U\C\V\x &= \y \notag \\ \x + \A^{-1}\U\C\V\x &= \A^{-1}\y \notag \\ \x &= \A^{-1}\y - \A^{-1}\U\C\V\x \tag{2} \\ \end{align}(A+UCV)xAx+UCVxx+A−1UCVxx​=y=y=A−1y=A−1y−A−1UCVx​(2)​

Here Vx\V \xVx plays the same role as v⊤xv^\top \xv⊤x in the previous example. And if we follow the same steps, we get

Vx=VA−1y−VA−1UCVx(Im+VA−1UC)Vx=VA−1yVx=(Im+VA−1UC)−1VA−1y\begin{align*} \V\x &= \V\A^{-1}\y - \V\A^{-1}\U\C\V\x \\ (\v{I}_m + \V\A^{-1}\U\C) \V\x &= \V\A^{-1}\y \\ \V\x &= (\v{I}_m + \V\A^{-1}\U\C)^{-1} \V\A^{-1}\y \\ \end{align*}Vx(Im​+VA−1UC)VxVx​=VA−1y−VA−1UCVx=VA−1y=(Im​+VA−1UC)−1VA−1y​

Here, the analog of 11+v⊤u\frac{1}{1 + v^\top u}1+v⊤u1​ is (Im+VA−1UC)−1(\v{I}_m + \V\A^{-1}\U\C)^{-1}(Im​+VA−1UC)−1. What was a scalar division is now a rank mmm matrix inversion. We are getting nearing our goal. Substituting Vx\V\xVx in (2)(2)(2) we finally get

x=A−1y−A−1UC(Im+VA−1UC)−1VA−1yx=(A−1−A−1U(C−1+VA−1U)−1VA−1)⏟≜(A+UCV)−1y\begin{align*} \x &= \A^{-1}\y - \A^{-1}\U\C(\v{I}_m + \V\A^{-1}\U\C)^{-1} \V\A^{-1}\y \\ \x &= \underbrace{(\A^{-1} - \A^{-1}\U(\C^{-1} + \V\A^{-1}\U)^{-1}\V\A^{-1})}_{\triangleq (\A + \U\C\\V)^{-1}} \y \end{align*}xx​=A−1y−A−1UC(Im​+VA−1UC)−1VA−1y=≜(A+UCV)−1(A−1−A−1U(C−1+VA−1U)−1VA−1)​​y​

This is exactly the Woodbury matrix identity. □\square□
